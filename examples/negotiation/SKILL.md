# Negotiation

Negotiation is a joint decision-making process between parties with different preferences. The goal is not necessarily compromise — it is reaching the best available outcome relative to your alternatives.

## Core Vocabulary

**BATNA** (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) — what you do if talks fail. Developed by Fisher & Ury (1981) in *Getting to Yes*. Your BATNA sets your effective floor: never accept a deal worse than your BATNA.

**Reservation price** (walk-away point) — the exact threshold at which you become indifferent between deal and no-deal. For a seller: minimum acceptable price. For a buyer: maximum willingness to pay. Always a specific number, not a range.

**ZOPA** (Zone of Possible Agreement) — the overlap between the buyer's reservation price and the seller's reservation price. A ZOPA exists only when the buyer's maximum ≥ the seller's minimum. No ZOPA = no deal is possible without changing the parties' underlying valuations.

**Aspiration point** — your target, set ambitiously above your reservation price. Research consistently shows higher aspirations → better outcomes, as long as they remain credible.

## Two Fundamental Modes

**Distributive** (claiming value): fixed-pie assumption, both sides compete for share. Appropriate for one-shot, single-issue transactions. Tactics: anchoring, concession patterns, time pressure.

**Integrative** (creating value): multiple issues let parties trade across differing priorities. Appropriate when there's a relationship, multiple issues, or repeated interaction. Tactics: interest revelation, logrolling, package deals.

Most real negotiations require both: expand the pie first (integrative), then divide it (distributive). Conflating the two phases is a common error.

## Anchoring

First offers have a correlation of ~0.50 with final outcomes (meta-analytic finding). The mechanism: anchors disproportionately direct attention — high anchors make buyers focus on the item's positive attributes; low anchors surface flaws.

Key findings:
- Making the first offer is generally advantageous when you have good information
- Extreme anchors increase impasse rates — the effect has diminishing returns
- Framing as an "offer" (gains framing) produces stronger anchoring than framing as a "request" (loss framing), because offers trigger lower concession aversion in the counterpart
- Counter-anchoring (making a counter-offer rather than just rejecting) neutralizes the effect more than ignoring the anchor

## The Harvard Method (Principled Negotiation)

Fisher & Ury's four-principle framework from *Getting to Yes* (1981):

1. **Separate the people from the problem** — relationship and substance are distinct. Treat the other party as a partner in solving a shared problem, not an adversary.
2. **Focus on interests, not positions** — a position is what someone demands; an interest is why they want it. Positions conflict; interests often don't.
3. **Invent options for mutual gain** — brainstorm before evaluating. The goal is to expand the solution space before claiming.
4. **Insist on objective criteria** — anchor final terms to market rates, precedent, independent standards. Reduces face-saving costs on both sides.

Limitation: principled negotiation is most effective when both parties adopt it. Against a purely distributive counterpart, it can leave value on the table.

## Psychological Biases That Destroy Value

See `references/psychology.md` for full coverage. Key biases:

**Fixed-pie bias** — default assumption that one party's gain is the other's loss. Persists even after subjects are told it's false. Suppresses integrative moves.

**Reactive devaluation** — automatically discounting proposals from the opposing side, regardless of their content. A proposal you'd accept from a neutral party gets rejected when it comes from an adversary. Identified by Ross & Stillinger (1991) in the context of arms control; confirmed in legal disputes, labor negotiations, and construction contracts.

**Overconfidence** — negotiators systematically overestimate the probability of agreement on their terms and underestimate the counterpart's reservation price.

**Loss aversion** — losses loom ~2× larger than equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky). Framing your proposal as preventing a loss rather than achieving a gain can shift the counterpart's risk preference.

## Concession Strategy

Concession patterns communicate intent:
- Decreasing concessions (large → small) signal approaching limits and tend to produce efficient agreements
- Equal-sized concessions suggest unlimited room; the counterpart will keep asking
- Unilateral concessions without reciprocity are interpreted as weakness

Time pressure accelerates concessions: more concessions are made in the final stages of negotiation than during the entire preceding discussion. Deadlines are therefore a structural power tool.

Contingent concessions ("I can do X if you do Y") are more effective than unconditional concessions because they explicitly invoke reciprocity.

## Power and Information

Power sources: BATNA strength, information, status, time (who can wait longer), relationship capital, and coalitions.

Information asymmetry: the side with more information about the other's reservation price has structural advantage. Countermeasures: ask questions, make multi-issue offers (the counterpart's reaction reveals priorities), run competitive processes.

BATNA improvement is the most reliable way to increase negotiating power — improving your outside option is often more valuable than improving your in-negotiation tactics.
