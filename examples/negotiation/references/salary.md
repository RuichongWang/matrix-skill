# Salary and Compensation Negotiation

## The Base Rate Problem

Most people don't negotiate. A CareerBuilder survey found 73% of employers expect salary negotiation, yet 54-55% of candidates don't attempt it. The asymmetry is extreme: you almost always have something to gain and very little to lose.

Among those who do negotiate: ~85% succeed in improving the offer. The average improvement is approximately $5,000 at the starting offer stage; across career lifetimes, an unmade $5,000 negotiation compounds (assuming 3% annual raises) to roughly $500,000+ in lifetime earnings loss.

## What Negotiation Strategies Actually Work

Research on salary negotiation outcomes (Gerhart & Rynes, 1991; subsequent replications):

**Competitive-collaborative hybrid wins**: Being competitive about outcome (specific high anchor, willingness to hold) while collaborative in process (non-adversarial tone, expressing enthusiasm for role) outperforms pure competition or pure collaboration. Pure compromisers and accommodators do not show statistically significant salary gains.

**Competing strategies vs. collaborating strategies**: In one study, competitive negotiators got higher salaries; collaborative negotiators reported higher satisfaction with the process. The implication: if you care more about outcome than relationship, lean competitive; if the relationship matters long-term, blend.

**Accommodation does not work**: Splitting differences, accepting the first offer, or framing requests as requests rather than expectations are not associated with salary gains.

## Anchoring in Salary Contexts

The first number anchors the outcome. Research suggests the first offer influences the final outcome by 50-80% of its deviation from the midpoint. Practical implications:
- Always try to hear the employer's offer first if you have limited market data
- If you know the market, anchor first and anchor high (but not absurdly so — extreme anchors increase impasse risk)
- Use a precise number rather than a round number (e.g., $127,000 rather than $125,000): precise numbers signal that the figure was calculated, not arbitrary, which increases its anchoring power

## The Role of Market Data

When objective salary information is available (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, industry surveys, recruiter data), gender and demographic differences in negotiation outcomes largely disappear. Market data neutralizes two sources of disadvantage:
- Lack of information about what's possible
- Social pressure to accept "reasonable" offers

Always enter salary negotiations with at least three data points: floor (25th percentile), midpoint (50th percentile), and ceiling (75th-90th percentile) for the role, level, and geography.

## Timing

Delay salary discussion until you have an offer. Before the offer, you have no leverage (they don't yet want you enough to pay); after the offer, you have maximum leverage (they've already decided they want you).

"What's your current salary?" — in many jurisdictions this is illegal to ask; even where legal, you can decline or redirect: "I'd rather focus on the value I'd bring and what the role is budgeted for." Anchoring on your current salary caps your negotiating range.

## Negotiating Beyond Base Salary

Compensation has multiple dimensions, each with different employer flexibility:

| Component | Typical flexibility | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| Base salary | Moderate | Often constrained by bands; ask about band ceiling |
| Signing bonus | High | One-time cost, doesn't compound — employers often have more room here |
| Equity / RSUs | High | Negotiable at hire; cliff/vesting schedule also negotiable |
| Title | Moderate | Can affect future earning power, worth pushing on |
| Remote policy | High | Increasingly a hard-dollar item (commuting, location flexibility) |
| Start date | High | Buying out unvested equity requires employer flexibility |
| Review cycle | Moderate | Earlier first review = faster reset of base |

When the employer claims the base is fixed, move to other dimensions rather than accepting "no."

## Gender and Negotiation: What the Evidence Actually Shows (2024)

Older research (pre-2015) found women negotiated less and were penalized more for negotiating. The 2024 picture is more nuanced:

- A 2024 study of business school graduates found women reported negotiating more frequently than men (54% vs. 44%)
- When job descriptions explicitly state salaries are negotiable, gender gaps in initiation disappear entirely
- Men in some studies showed anxiety responses to women negotiating assertively, leading to reduced collaboration — but this is an employer bias problem, not a negotiator strategy problem
- When domain is "masculine" (e.g., motorcycle parts), men outperform; when domain is neutral or "feminine" (e.g., jewelry components), no significant difference

**Practical takeaway**: Frame your negotiation as expected, not exceptional. Normalize it in the conversation: "I'm excited about the role — I'd like to discuss the compensation to make sure we're aligned." This framing reduces the social penalty risk for all negotiators.

## The "No" Isn't Final

Initial rejection of a negotiated request is not the end. Tactics after a "no":
- Ask what would need to change for the offer to reach your number
- Ask about timeline for a first review ("If we can't reach $X now, when could we revisit?")
- Propose a performance-contingent structure ("If I hit [metric] in 6 months, can we revisit salary then?")
- Accept on condition of early review — get it in writing

Companies rarely rescind offers because a candidate negotiated. The downside risk is nearly zero; the upside is substantial.
