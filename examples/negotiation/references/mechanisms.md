# Negotiation Mechanisms: Integrative Tactics and Value Creation

## The Integrative-Distributive Sequence

Effective negotiation separates value creation from value claiming. The error most negotiators make: they go distributive immediately, anchoring on positions before the full solution space is mapped. This produces agreements that are "good enough" but not Pareto-optimal — both parties could have done better.

The recommended sequence:
1. Reveal interests (not positions) through questions and conditional disclosures
2. Build a large issue set — more issues = more trade opportunities
3. Make package proposals (bundles) rather than sequential single-issue offers
4. Claim value on the resulting agreement

## Logrolling

Logrolling is the exchange of concessions on issues where the parties have different priority orderings. If Party A cares more about delivery timing and Party B cares more about price, they can trade: B gets a lower price, A gets faster delivery. Both do better than if they split each issue 50/50.

Prerequisite: you must know (or infer) the counterpart's priority ordering. Strategies:
- Ask directly: "Which of these issues matters most to you?"
- Make a multi-issue offer and observe the reaction — the issue they push back on hardest is their priority
- Offer two packages designed around different priority assumptions; which they prefer reveals their ordering

## Post-Settlement Settlement (PSS)

After reaching an agreement, both parties optionally submit their agreement to a neutral third party, who attempts to find a Pareto-superior deal — one that makes both parties better off. If found, it replaces the original agreement. If not found, the original stands.

PSS decouples the pressure to close from the quality of the final deal. It eliminates the problem of leaving joint gains on the table because neither party wants to reopen a hard-won agreement. Raiffa introduced this concept formally.

## Contingent Agreements

When parties disagree about future events (will the product perform? will the market grow?), contingent agreements make deal terms conditional on the outcome: "We'll pay $X if metric Y is achieved, $Z otherwise." Benefits:
- Lets parties bet according to their beliefs rather than splitting the difference
- Aligns incentives post-deal
- Can unlock deals that would otherwise fail due to incompatible forecasts

Common in M&A (earnouts), film/book deals (performance clauses), and construction (milestone payments).

## Multi-Party Negotiations

Coalition dynamics: parties in multi-party negotiations tend to form minimum winning coalitions (the smallest group needed to get a majority). Being pivotal — needed by any winning coalition — maximizes your leverage.

In multi-party contexts, anchoring effects still apply, but they get diluted by the simultaneous influence of multiple parties. The agenda-setter (who controls which issues are addressed in what order) gains structural power.

## Negotiation Audits

Before a negotiation, map:
- Your reservation price and aspiration point
- Your BATNA and the counterpart's probable BATNA
- The full issue set (price, timing, terms, relationship, optionality)
- Your priority ordering across issues
- Their probable priority ordering
- The likely ZOPA

This pre-work is where most negotiation value is created or destroyed. Studies consistently show that under-prepared negotiators leave more on the table than those who prepare less strategically in-session.

## The Pareto Frontier

A Pareto-efficient outcome is one where no further improvement for one party is possible without making the other party worse off. Most negotiations — even successful ones — produce agreements inside the Pareto frontier. The gap between the actual outcome and the frontier is the "negotiation surplus" left uncaptured.

The primary culprit for surplus loss: fixed-pie bias causing parties not to explore trades across issues. Secondary culprit: positional bargaining locking in suboptimal solutions before the solution space is fully mapped.
