# Negotiation Psychology: Biases, Framing, and Cognitive Traps

## Fixed-Pie Bias

The default assumption that negotiation is zero-sum — that any gain for one party is a proportional loss for the other. This bias persists even when:
- Subjects are explicitly told it's false
- The structure of the negotiation makes integration obviously available
- Experienced negotiators are involved

Research by Thompson & Hastie (1990) found that a majority of negotiators operate under fixed-pie assumptions even in multi-issue negotiations with clear logrolling potential. The bias does not disappear with experience alone — it requires deliberate reframing.

**Countermeasure**: Before negotiating, explicitly ask "what issues does each party value differently?" If the answer isn't "none," there's probably integrative potential.

## Reactive Devaluation

Proposals are automatically discounted when they originate from an opposing party. The same concession that would be accepted from a neutral party or initially requested as your own position gets rejected when offered by the adversary.

Ross & Stillinger (1991) documented this in arms control negotiations. It has since been replicated in:
- Legal settlements: parties reject settlements proposed by opposing counsel that they would accept from a mediator
- Construction disputes: contractor proposals scored lower when the identity of the proposer was disclosed
- Labor negotiations

**Practical implication**: Use neutral mediators or objective criteria to propose difficult concessions. Strip the adversarial framing from a proposal before evaluating it.

**Countermeasure for the other side**: Ask yourself: "Would I accept this if a neutral party proposed it?" If yes, reactive devaluation is operating.

## Loss Aversion

From Kahneman & Tversky's Prospect Theory: losses are weighted approximately 2× more heavily than equivalent gains. In negotiation:
- Framing a concession as "avoiding a loss" can make it more palatable than the equivalent "forgoing a gain"
- Parties are more risk-seeking when below their reference point (willing to gamble rather than accept a certain loss)
- The reference point is malleable — anchors set it

Negotiators can exploit this by framing proposals in loss terms relative to the counterpart's reference point ("at current rates, you're leaving $X on the table every month") rather than gain terms.

## Overconfidence and the Winner's Curse

Negotiators consistently overestimate the probability of outcomes that favor them. This manifests as:
- Setting aspirations too close to the ZOPA's center rather than the far end
- Underestimating the counterpart's reservation price
- In auctions: the "winner's curse" — the winning bidder likely overbid, since they beat all other informed parties

**Countermeasure**: Before negotiating, explicitly model the counterpart's perspective. Ask: "What's the best deal they could walk away to?" This tends to calibrate downward the expectation of easy wins.

## Anchoring (Psychological Mechanism)

Why anchors work: insufficient adjustment from an initial value. Even when people know an anchor is random (a spinning wheel, a product's bar code), they adjust toward it from wherever they land. The anchor creates a reference point for the adjustment, but people systematically stop adjusting too early.

In negotiation, anchors are not random — they are strategically placed. The effect is stronger when:
- The anchor is delivered with confidence
- The recipient has limited prior knowledge of the domain
- The anchor is accompanied by justification (even weak justification amplifies the effect)

Counter-anchoring is more effective than ignoring. Simply receiving and processing an extreme first offer anchors you; immediately responding with a counter-anchor re-centers the frame.

## Ego Depletion and Cognitive Load

Negotiation quality degrades under cognitive load, fatigue, and time pressure. Studies show:
- Complex negotiations late in the day produce worse outcomes than the same negotiations in the morning
- Multitasking during negotiation (distracted attention) increases susceptibility to anchoring
- Artificial deadlines accelerate concessions disproportionately in the party more susceptible to time pressure

**Practical implication**: Negotiate important deals when you are cognitively fresh. Avoid artificial time pressure you haven't imposed yourself.

## The Endowment Effect

People value things more once they own them. In negotiation, this means:
- Sellers routinely set reservation prices above market value because of ownership
- Parties in multi-issue negotiations may over-weight their current positions simply because those are the positions they hold

**Countermeasure**: Periodically adopt the counterpart's perspective as a role-playing exercise before finalizing positions.

## Attribution Errors in Multi-Round Negotiations

Negotiators attribute their own concessions to strategic choice and external circumstance, while attributing the counterpart's concessions to weakness or pressure. This asymmetric attribution:
- Leads to demands for more concessions after receiving one ("they wouldn't have conceded unless they had room")
- Causes reciprocity to feel less obligatory ("I only conceded because of X, not because they pressured me")

The practical result: negotiators receive less credit for concessions than they expected, and give less credit than their counterparts expected.
