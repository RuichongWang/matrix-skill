# Biology

## What biology is

Biology is the study of living organisms — what they're made of, how they work, how they
reproduce, how they interact with each other, and how they change over time. It spans from
molecules inside a single cell to global ecosystems. The entire field rests on one unifying
idea: all life on Earth shares a common origin and is shaped by evolution.

---

## How it works / Core mechanisms

**Cells are the basic unit of life.**
Every living thing is made of cells. A bacterium is one cell. A human is ~37 trillion.
Cells contain the machinery to take in energy, make proteins, and copy themselves.
All cells come from pre-existing cells — life never spontaneously generates.

**DNA is the instruction manual.**
DNA is a double-stranded molecule made of four bases (A, T, C, G). The sequence encodes genes.
Genes are transcribed into RNA, then translated into proteins. Proteins do almost everything:
they catalyze reactions (enzymes), provide structure, carry signals, defend against pathogens.
The human genome has ~3 billion base pairs and ~20,000 protein-coding genes.

**Energy flows through living systems.**
Life requires constant energy input. Plants capture sunlight via photosynthesis:
CO₂ + H₂O + light → glucose + O₂ (in chloroplasts, via two stages: light reactions + Calvin cycle).
Animals eat to get that stored energy back. All cells burn glucose via cellular respiration:
glucose + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + ATP (the cell's energy currency). ATP powers nearly everything cells do.

**Evolution shapes all life over time.**
Species change across generations through natural selection: individuals with traits better
suited to their environment survive and reproduce more. Over millions of years, this produces
new species from common ancestors. Evidence comes from fossils, DNA comparisons, and direct
observation (bacteria evolving antibiotic resistance in real time).

**Cells communicate and regulate themselves.**
Cells don't act alone — they send and receive chemical signals (hormones, neurotransmitters,
cytokines). Gene expression is tightly regulated: not all genes are active in all cells at all
times. A liver cell and a neuron have the same DNA but behave completely differently because
different genes are switched on.

## Key areas

**Molecular biology** — DNA, RNA, proteins; how genetic information is stored, copied, and expressed.
Core techniques: PCR, gene sequencing, CRISPR gene editing.

**Cell biology** — structure and function of cells; organelles (nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes);
how cells divide (mitosis, meiosis), move, and die.

**Genetics** — inheritance of traits; Mendelian laws (dominant/recessive); mutations;
population genetics (how allele frequencies change across generations).

**Evolutionary biology** — how species change and diversify; natural selection, genetic drift,
speciation; phylogenetics (reconstructing the tree of life from DNA).

**Ecology** — how organisms interact with each other and their environment; food webs, nutrient
cycles, population dynamics, climate effects on ecosystems.

**Neuroscience** — how nervous systems work; neurons, synapses, action potentials; brain structure
and function; behavior.

**Microbiology** — bacteria, viruses, fungi, and other microorganisms; infection, immunity, the
microbiome (the ~38 trillion bacteria living in/on a human body).

**Developmental biology** — how a single fertilized cell becomes a complex multicellular organism;
stem cells, differentiation, morphogenesis.

## Important facts to know

- All known life uses the same genetic code (DNA → RNA → protein) — strong evidence of common ancestry
- Mitochondria were once free-living bacteria absorbed by larger cells ~1.5 billion years ago (endosymbiosis)
- Viruses are not cells — they're packages of genetic material that hijack host cells to replicate
- ~99% of species that ever lived are now extinct
- The human body contains more bacterial cells than human cells (though bacteria are much smaller)
- CRISPR-Cas9 (discovered ~2012) allows precise editing of any DNA sequence; already used in human medicine
- Photosynthesis produces essentially all the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere
- Neurons communicate via electrical signals (action potentials) and chemical signals (neurotransmitters)
- Cancer is fundamentally a disease of uncontrolled cell division caused by mutations in DNA
- The Tree of Life has three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya — humans are Eukarya
- Life on Earth is ~3.8 billion years old; multicellular life only ~600 million years old
- RNA likely preceded DNA in early life — the "RNA world" hypothesis
- Epigenetics: gene expression can be altered by environment without changing the DNA sequence itself

## Key vocabulary

**Gene** — a stretch of DNA that encodes a functional product (usually a protein)
**Allele** — a variant form of a gene (e.g. the gene for eye color has alleles for blue, brown, etc.)
**Protein** — a chain of amino acids folded into a 3D shape; the workhorse molecules of life
**Enzyme** — a protein that catalyzes (speeds up) a specific chemical reaction
**ATP** — adenosine triphosphate; the universal energy currency of cells
**Mutation** — a change in DNA sequence; can be neutral, harmful, or beneficial
**Phenotype** — the observable traits of an organism (what you see)
**Genotype** — the genetic makeup underlying those traits (what's in the DNA)
**Homeostasis** — maintaining stable internal conditions (e.g. body temperature, blood sugar)
**Meiosis** — cell division that produces sex cells (sperm/eggs) with half the normal chromosome count
**Mitosis** — cell division that produces two identical daughter cells (growth, repair)
**CRISPR** — a molecular tool derived from bacterial immune systems; used to edit DNA precisely
**Microbiome** — the community of microorganisms living in/on a host organism

## What's still unknown / active frontiers

- **Origin of life** — how the first self-replicating molecules arose from chemistry; genuinely unsolved
- **Consciousness** — how neural activity gives rise to subjective experience; no consensus
- **Protein folding** — AlphaFold (2021) transformed structure prediction, but the full rules aren't understood
- **Dark proteome** — a large fraction of human proteins have unknown functions
- **Aging** — why organisms age; multiple competing theories (telomere shortening, senescent cells, mitochondrial damage)
- **Microbiome and health** — the gut microbiome clearly affects health, but causal mechanisms are mostly unclear
- **Gene regulation** — ~98% of the genome is non-coding; much of it regulates genes in ways still being mapped
